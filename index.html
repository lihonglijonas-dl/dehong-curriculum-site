1. Home

A Future-focused Curriculum and Assessment Model for Junior Secondary Chinese at Xi’an Dehong

Theme–Text Type–Task–Feedback–Evidence Model

Thesis Statement

This project proposes a Theme–Text Type–Task–Feedback–Evidence Model that aligns curriculum aims, text-based learning tasks, feedback processes and assessment evidence in junior secondary Chinese.

The model integrates curriculum design and assessment design by connecting themes, text types, learning tasks, feedback, portfolio evidence and teacher-governed AI support.

Scope Note

This is a design proposal based on public school materials, official curriculum documents, public practice artefacts and research literature. It does not claim access to Xi’an Dehong’s internal rubrics, grading mechanisms, reporting procedures, official unit plans, teacher assessment records or student performance data.

2. Context

Context

This section introduces the rationale, disciplinary focus and evidence limits for the proposal.

Why Xi'an Dehong?

Xi'an Dehong's Middle School section corresponds to junior secondary education and covers Grades 6 to 9. Public-facing school materials present it as built on China's nine-year compulsory national curriculum while combining this with CML/EML, inquiry-based learning, cross-disciplinary themes and language-development text types. It is used here as a specific case context, not as a representative sample of all Chinese junior secondary schools.

Why junior secondary Chinese?

Chinese provides a subject space for language development, textual understanding, cultural expression and written/oral communication. Xi'an Dehong's public language-development framework lists narrative, explanatory, argumentative and practical text types, making text type a suitable organising element for curriculum and assessment design. Screenshots from Xi'an Dehong's official WeChat articles are treated only as public practice artefacts.

Evidence boundary

This is a design proposal based on public evidence. It does not include internal Chinese rubrics, grade-weighting rules, reporting mechanisms, official unit plans, teacher assessment records or student performance data. Public practice artefacts inform design possibilities; they do not verify internal implementation.

4. Frameworks & Tensions

Frameworks & Tensions

This section sets out the analytical lenses and key design tensions that inform the proposed curriculum and assessment model.

Framework lenses

Curriculum as Content / Process / Product

Content refers to selected Chinese learning content, including text types, reading materials, writing tasks and thematic activities. Process refers to how students learn through reading, discussion, drafting, feedback, revision, performance and reflection. Product refers to the evidence students generate, such as reading responses, rewritten texts, oral presentations, performance artefacts, portfolios and reflective statements. This framework helps analyse whether curriculum content, learning process and learning outcomes are connected.

Convergent / Divergent Assessment

Convergent assessment checks predetermined targets such as vocabulary, textual comprehension, language accuracy and basic writing conventions. Divergent assessment captures open evidence such as reading journals, discussion, text rewriting, performance, portfolios and self-reflection. Junior secondary Chinese still needs convergent assessment, but a future-focused model also needs divergent evidence of interpretation, expression, revision and process-based learning.

Design tensions

Core competencies and academic quality require clearer evidence

The 2022 curriculum reform strengthens core competencies, academic quality standards and teaching-assessment guidance. A single final test may remain useful, but it cannot provide enough evidence for broader outcomes such as interpretation, expression, transfer and reflection.

Rich language practices need a clearer curriculum-assessment chain

Public materials and screenshots show design possibilities such as deep reading, classic-text activation, literary performance and collaborative expression. These artefacts do not prove formal assessment mechanisms. The design issue is how to connect goals, tasks, feedback, revision and evidence.

AI offers feedback opportunities but raises validity and integrity risks

AI may support reading, feedback and process recording. It also creates risks around authorship, genuine understanding, privacy, over-reliance and evidence authenticity. The model therefore needs teacher-governed AI use.

5. Reform Principles

Reform Principles

These principle cards create a concise bridge between the evidence review and the proposed model.

Align aims, tasks and evidence

Principle: Curriculum aims, learning tasks and assessment evidence must be aligned.

Problem addressed: If assessment only records final answers or final marks, it cannot show how students develop textual understanding, expression, revision or reflective judgement.

Design implication: Each major Chinese task should specify its learning outcome, evidence form and success criteria.

Build a continuous learning sequence

Principle: Reading, writing, feedback, revision, performance and reflection should be organised as one continuous learning sequence.

Problem addressed: Rich language activities are weaker as assessment evidence if they remain disconnected events.

Design implication: Activities should be linked to explicit goals, feedback cycles and student-produced artefacts.

Balance convergent and divergent assessment

Principle: Assessment should balance foundational checks with open performance evidence.

Problem addressed: A narrowly test-driven model misses interpretation, expression, revision and process evidence; a purely activity-based model lacks standards.

Design implication: The model should retain checks of vocabulary, comprehension, language accuracy and basic writing conventions while adding rewriting, oral explanation, performance, portfolio and reflection.

AI-assisted, teacher-governed

Principle: AI may assist feedback and evidence generation, but teacher judgement must govern standards, evidence validity and academic integrity.

Problem addressed: AI can support reading prompts and draft feedback, but it can also create authorship, originality, privacy and over-reliance risks.

Design implication: AI use should be documented through AI-use statements, revision traces and teacher review. AI must not replace student textual judgement or final teacher assessment.

These principles lead into the proposed Theme–Text Type–Task–Feedback–Evidence Model.

7. Example Unit

Example Unit: Making Classical Texts Come Alive

Use this section to demonstrate how the model could operate in a bounded, classroom-scale design example.

Proposed Example Notice

This is a proposed example unit. It does not describe an existing internal Xi'an Dehong unit. Public WeChat screenshots are treated only as public practice artefacts.

Unit snapshot

Unit theme Contemporary expression of classical texts / literature and self-expression
Text selection A classical narrative, poem, prose extract or dramatic passage appropriate to junior secondary Chinese
Text types Narrative text, poetic/prose extract, dramatic text, short commentary
Core task Conduct close reading, rewrite one scene or passage, stage the rewritten version, and produce a reflective commentary
Learning process Pre-reading, close reading, discussion, rewriting, feedback, revision, performance, reflection
Assessment structure Diagnostic assessment, formative assessment, summative performance assessment
AI use position Reading support, draft feedback, process organisation
Teacher role Standard-setting, feedback judgement, evidence review, ethical governance
Final evidence Rewritten script, performance material, commentary, revision note, AI-use statement, portfolio

Seven-step learning sequence

  1. 1

    Diagnostic pre-reading

    Students submit an initial reading response, question list and brief prior-knowledge statement. Evidence: pre-reading response, question list, initial interpretation.

  2. 2

    Close reading

    Students analyse character, plot, language, cultural context and central conflict. Evidence: annotated text and reading notes.

  3. 3

    Controlled AI-supported preparation

    Students may organise background information, generate pre-reading questions or clarify keywords under controlled limits. Evidence: AI-use record and preparation notes.

  4. 4

    Rewriting and role interpretation

    Students rewrite a selected section into a performable version and explain their textual choices. Evidence: draft script and role explanation.

  5. 5

    Peer and teacher feedback

    Students receive feedback on textual accuracy, language choices, interpretation, originality and performance feasibility. Evidence: peer feedback and teacher feedback records.

  6. 6

    Revision and performance

    Students revise the script and complete a group presentation. Evidence: revised script, performance record and revision trace.

  7. 7

    Reflection and portfolio compilation

    Students explain how their understanding changed, why they revised specific parts, and how AI was used or rejected. Evidence: reflective commentary, AI-use statement and portfolio selection.

Success criteria

Dimension Indicator Evidence source
Textual understanding Whether the rewriting and performance are grounded in close reading rather than surface imitation Annotated text, reading notes, oral explanation
Language accuracy and expression Whether language choices fit character, context, genre and communicative purpose Rewritten script, performance material, commentary
Interpretive justification Whether students can explain why they rewrote or performed the text in a particular way Role explanation, oral explanation, reflective commentary
Revision quality Whether peer, teacher or AI-supported feedback leads to meaningful improvement Draft, feedback record, revised script
Academic integrity Whether AI use is transparent and does not replace student judgement AI-use statement, version comparison

AI risk controls

Risk In Chinese assessment Control Evidence of compliance
Originality risk Students may submit AI-generated text as their own work. AI may not generate the final rewritten script; require drafts, revision traces and AI-use statements. Draft history, revision trace, AI-use statement, before-and-after comparison
Hollow understanding risk Students may produce correct-looking responses without genuine textual understanding. Keep close reading central and add oral interpretation or live questioning where understanding is uncertain. Annotated text, reading notes, oral explanation
Feedback dependency risk Students may copy AI feedback without exercising judgement. Require students to explain whether they accepted, modified or rejected suggestions and why. AI-use statement, revision note, before-and-after comparison
Evidence authenticity risk Students may fabricate process records or incomplete evidence trails. Require coherent drafts, feedback records and revision explanations to be read alongside final performance. Draft script, feedback records, revision trace, reflective commentary
Privacy risk Students may upload personal information or unauthorised materials into AI tools. Restrict the type of information entered into AI tools and require transparent AI-use records. AI-use statement and teacher review of permitted use

Interpretive Note

Evidence should be interpreted through triangulation. The portfolio should be a selected evidence set organised around learning outcomes rather than a collection of all materials. The AI-use statement is not proof of Chinese competence. Chinese competence remains evidenced through textual interpretation, rewriting choices, oral explanation, performance and reflective commentary.
